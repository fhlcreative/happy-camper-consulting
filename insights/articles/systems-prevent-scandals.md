# **Systems Prevent Scandals**

Why Camp Leaders Need Structure Before Something Goes Wrong

By Jeff Rorabaugh, Founder &amp; Principal Consultant

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Introduction**

Most camp scandals do not begin with one dramatic decision.

They begin with drift.

A concern that felt too small to report.

A boundary that was a little too loose.<br />

A supervisor who assumed someone else was handling it.<br />

A director who heard about a problem too late.</p>

By the time a scandal is visible, the real failure usually happened much earlier.

Not in one moment of chaos.

But in a long chain of unclear expectations, weak reporting, and informal leadership.

That is why strong camps do not wait for a crisis to tighten their systems.

They build structure early — because systems prevent scandals.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Scandals Grow in the Gaps**

When camp leaders hear the word scandal, they often picture a headline.

A public accusation.

A board crisis.<br />

A parent firestorm.<br />

A legal mess that damages trust for years.</p>

But long before the headline, there were usually smaller gaps:

Staff were not sure what needed immediate escalation.

Supervisors corrected tone differently from one cabin to the next.

Boundaries were assumed rather than reinforced.

Documentation was inconsistent.

People relied on good intentions instead of clear process.

Scandals grow where ambiguity is tolerated.

That is true in churches.

In nonprofits.<br />

In schools.<br />

And it is absolutely true in camps.</p>

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Good People Are Not a System**

One of the hardest truths for faith-based and mission-driven leaders to accept is this:

Good people are not enough.

A camp may have caring counselors, sincere supervisors, and a deeply committed director.

That matters.

Character matters.

Intentions matter.<br />

Culture matters.</p>

But when there is no clear system for reporting, supervision, correction, and follow-through, even good teams become vulnerable.

Why?

Because under pressure, people do not rise to their ideals.

They fall to the level of their systems.

If the system is unclear, responses become inconsistent.

If responses are inconsistent, trust begins to erode.

And once trust erodes, scandal risk rises fast.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **What Actually Prevents Scandal**

Scandal prevention is not primarily about public relations.

It is not about having the right statement ready after something happens.

It is about reducing the likelihood that hidden problems can grow.

That requires structure in four areas.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### **1. Reporting Culture**

Staff must know what to report, when to report it, and to whom.

More importantly, they must believe that reporting early is a sign of health — not disloyalty, weakness, or overreaction.

As I often tell camp leaders: I don't like surprises.

That phrase is not about control.

It is about stewardship.

A good director wants to hear about a concern from a counselor today — not from a parent next week.

If a staff member thinks:

Maybe this is too small.

Maybe I should wait.

Maybe I will get someone in trouble.

— then your reporting culture is not yet strong enough.

Healthy camps normalize phrases like:

"If something makes you go... hmmmmm, then it is important to communicate it."

That kind of language lowers the threshold for early communication.

And early communication prevents hidden problems from maturing into public crises.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### **2. Supervisor Calibration**

Many camp problems do not escalate because there was no policy.

They escalate because the policy was applied differently by different leaders.

One supervisor addresses a concern immediately.

Another minimizes it.<br />

One follows up carefully.<br />

Another assumes the issue has passed.</p>

This is how drift becomes risk.

Supervisors are the translation layer of camp culture.

They turn [values into behavior](https://www.happycamperconsultant.com/insights/article.html?slug=a-behavior-is-a-value-in-action).

They turn policy into practice.<br />

They turn leadership intent into lived experience.</p>

If supervisors are not calibrated, every part of camp begins operating by a different standard.

That is dangerous.

Strong camps build regular calibration rhythms:

What are we seeing?

What needs escalation?

Where is tone drifting?

Are we correcting consistently?

Scandals often begin where leaders are left to interpret high-stakes moments on their own.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### **3. Boundary Clarity**

Most camps have boundary rules.

The real question is whether staff can remember and apply them in real time.

Do they know what to do when a camper wants one-on-one time?

Do they know what to do when another staff member crosses a line?<br />

Do they know what to do when something feels off, but they cannot yet prove it?</p>

Boundary clarity is not built by handing out a handbook once in June.

It is built through repeated language, scenario practice, and reinforcement.

That is why [safety culture is more than policies](https://www.happycamperconsultant.com/insights/article.html?slug=why-culture-eats-strategy).

Policies matter. But practiced boundaries protect people.

A camp that says, "our staff know better," is already exposed.

A camp that says, "our staff are trained, reminded, coached, and corrected clearly," is much more likely to remain healthy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

### **4. Documentation and Follow-Through**

Scandals thrive where memory replaces documentation.

When concerns are handled informally:

When incidents are discussed but not recorded:<br />

When leadership assumes "we dealt with that already":</p>

— the organization loses clarity.

Documentation is not a lack of trust.

It is trustworthiness in written form.

It protects campers.

It protects staff.<br />

It protects directors.<br />

It protects the mission.</p>

As we explored in [Operational Reliability ](https://www.happycamperconsultant.com/insights/article.html?slug=operational-reliability-in-overnight-camps)in Overnight Camps, reliability is built through repeatable systems — not heroic effort.

If your camp handles high-stakes issues differently depending on who is on duty, you do not yet have a reliable system.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Why Faith-Based Camps Must Be Especially Careful**

Faith-based camps often carry extraordinary strengths:

deep trust

strong community

shared mission

high relational warmth

Those strengths are real.

They are gifts.

But they can also create blind spots when leaders assume shared values automatically produce safe systems.

They do not.

Mission does not eliminate risk.

Sincerity does not eliminate drift.<br />

Warmth does not eliminate ambiguity.</p>

In fact, environments that feel like family can become more vulnerable when hard conversations are avoided in the name of unity.

Healthy Christian leadership does not hide problems to protect appearances.

It brings things into the light early, clearly, and with courage.

Because the goal is not image management.

The goal is faithful stewardship.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Warning Signs Your Systems Are Too Weak**

If any of these feel familiar, your camp may be depending too heavily on instinct:

Staff are unclear about what requires immediate escalation.

Supervisors handle the same issue in noticeably different ways.

Documentation happens inconsistently or only after serious incidents.

Leadership learns about concerns late.

Boundaries are "understood" but rarely practiced out loud.

People hesitate to report because they fear being seen as dramatic.

The director is the only person everyone trusts to handle sensitive situations correctly.

These are not reasons to panic.

They are reasons to build.

Strong systems are not created by shame.

They are created by honesty, clarity, and repetition.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Practical First Steps**

You do not have to rebuild your whole camp in one week.

But you do need to strengthen the right things first.

Clarify your escalation triggers. Put in writing what must be reported immediately, what must be documented, and who needs to know.

Train supervisors together. Do not assume alignment. Build it intentionally.

Practice real scenarios. If staff only hear the policy, but never practice the moment, they will hesitate under pressure.

Use simple reporting language. Make it easy for young staff to communicate early.

Audit your weak spots. Look at where problems tend to hide: cabins, informal counselor culture, unstructured time, and leadership handoffs.

Systems before scale.

Clarity before crisis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

## **Why This Matters**

Camps do not lose trust all at once.

They lose it one unclear moment at a time.

And camps do not build trust all at once either.

They build it through repeated, visible, reliable structure.

When reporting is clear, supervisors are calibrated, boundaries are reinforced, and documentation is consistent, camps become better equipped to protect both people and mission.

That is not bureaucracy.

That is hospitality.

That is stewardship.

That is how healthy camps avoid preventable pain.

Scandals rarely begin with one catastrophic failure.

They grow where systems are too weak to stop small problems early.

Healthy camps don't happen by accident. They are built — on purpose.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
